Now we enter **Ideate**.

For Crazy 8s, we should resist designing eight polished screens. The method is meant to force **eight distinct solution directions in eight minutes** for one focused HMW challenge. Some ideas should overlap, some should feel strange, and some should contradict each other.

Because our research produced eight consolidated design challenges, doing eight ideas for all eight immediately would create 64 ideas and become noise. We should start with the foundational challenge:

> **How might we help users experience proposal preparation, review, approval, activation, implementation, reporting, and closure as one understandable project journey?**

Everything in the current specifications—from UC-4 through UC-8—depends on solving this coherently. 

# Crazy 8s: The Continuous Project Journey

## 1. The Project Timeline

### Core idea

Represent the entire extension project as one horizontal or vertical lifecycle:

**Proposal Preparation**
↓
**Under Review**
↓
**Approved**
↓
**Preparing for Implementation**
↓
**Ongoing**
↓
**Reporting**
↓
**Closing**
↓
**Completed**

The current stage is emphasized.

Completed stages remain visible.

Future stages are visible but secondary.

### What the user sees

> **Community Digital Literacy Program**

> Current stage: Director/Admin Review

> ✓ Proposal submitted
> ✓ RET Chair endorsed
> ● Director/Admin review
> ○ Project readiness
> ○ Implementation
> ○ Reporting
> ○ Closure

### Why it might work

It directly solves lifecycle fragmentation.

### Risk

A timeline can become decorative.

If users can see:

> **Project Readiness**

but cannot understand what is blocking it, we have only made the confusion prettier.

### Crazy 8s verdict

**Strong orientation concept, weak as a complete solution.**

---

## 2. The “What Happens Next?” Card

### Core idea

Forget the full lifecycle.

Show only the user's immediate reality.

Every project has one dominant card:

> **What happens next?**

Example:

> **Waiting for Director/Admin Review**

> Your proposal was endorsed by the RET Chair on July 3.

> **Your action:** None

> **Waiting for:** Extension Services Office

> **Next:** If approved, the project will proceed to implementation preparation.

### Why it might work

Users often do not need the entire workflow.

They need:

> “What do I do now?”

### Risk

Users may lose the larger context.

They understand the next step but not the journey.

### Crazy 8s verdict

**Extremely strong for reducing cognitive load.**

This should probably survive concept selection even if the final design uses another primary structure.

---

## 3. The Project Story

### Core idea

Instead of a process diagram, represent the project as a chronological narrative.

> **Today**

> Your revised proposal is awaiting Director/Admin review.

> **July 3**

> You submitted Revision 2.

> **July 1**

> The Director/Admin requested revisions.

> “Please revise the implementation timeline.”

> **June 27**

> Your RET Chair endorsed the proposal.

> **June 25**

> You submitted your proposal.

### Why it might work

Users naturally understand:

> What happened?

better than abstract workflow states.

It also combines:

* history;
* feedback;
* documents;
* decisions;
* handoffs.

### Risk

Long-running projects could produce overwhelming timelines.

### Crazy 8s verdict

**Strong for traceability and revision-heavy workflows.**

---

## 4. The Journey Map Workspace

### Core idea

Turn each project into a visual map.

The project has regions:

**Prepare** → **Get Approved** → **Get Ready** → **Implement** → **Complete**

Each region contains tasks.

For example:

### Get Ready

✓ Proposal approved
✓ Special Orders uploaded
○ Valid MOA assigned
○ Reporting schedule established

> **Blocked: Waiting for valid MOA**

### Why it might work

It connects the project journey with actionable requirements.

### Risk

Could become visually complex, especially:

* on mobile;
* at high zoom;
* for screen-reader users;
* for long projects.

### Accessibility concern

A visual map must have an equivalent semantic structure.

### Crazy 8s verdict

**Powerful, but potentially overdesigned.**

---

## 5. The Project Checklist

### Core idea

Treat the entire project like a progressive checklist.

> **Project Progress: 7 of 14 milestones complete**

### Proposal

✓ Project information completed
✓ Proposal documents submitted

### Evaluation

✓ RET Chair endorsement
● Director/Admin review

### Before Implementation

○ Special Orders
○ MOA
○ Reporting schedule

### Implementation

○ Project activated
○ Progress Report 1
○ Progress Report 2

### Completion

○ Terminal Report
○ Final Accomplishment Report
○ Project closure

### Why it might work

Extremely understandable.

Users can see:

* completed;
* current;
* incomplete;
* future.

### Risk

It may imply that every project follows the exact same sequence.

It may also turn complex institutional decisions into simplistic “tasks.”

### Crazy 8s verdict

**Excellent for preparation and readiness, weaker for review and decision-making.**

---

## 6. The Project Control Center

### Core idea

Each project has one workspace with five questions:

### Where is it?

> Director/Admin Review

### Do I need to do anything?

> No action required

### What are we waiting for?

> Director/Admin decision

### What happened recently?

> RET Chair endorsed the proposal on July 3

### What happens next?

> If approved, the project proceeds to implementation preparation

Below that:

**Documents | Feedback | Reports | History | Team**

### Why it might work

It organizes the experience around user questions rather than system entities.

### Risk

Could become another dashboard full of cards.

### Crazy 8s verdict

**Very strong mental-model alignment.**

---

## 7. The “One Project, One Page” Concept

### Core idea

Remove separate user-facing modules for:

* proposals;
* evaluations;
* Special Orders;
* reports.

The user opens:

> **My Projects**

Then:

> **Community Digital Literacy Program**

Everything happens inside that project.

Proposal files are in the project.

Evaluation feedback is in the project.

Special Orders are in the project.

MOA information is in the project.

Reports are in the project.

History is in the project.

### Why it might work

This is the purest expression of our empathy finding:

> **Users experience one project.**

### Risk

The page could become enormous.

Director/Admin and RET Chairs still need cross-project operational views.

### Crazy 8s verdict

**Probably the strongest information-architecture idea.**

It does not mean literally one endlessly scrolling page. It means:

> **The project is the primary container of the experience.**

---

## 8. The Conversational Project Guide

### Core idea

The system communicates like a procedural assistant.

The user opens a project:

> **Your proposal has been approved.**

> Before implementation can begin, three requirements must be completed.

> ✓ Special Orders complete

> ○ MOA assignment — Extension Services Office

> ○ Reporting schedule — Extension Services Office

> **You do not need to do anything right now.**

Later:

> **Action required**

> Your proposal was returned for revision.

> The Director/Admin requested changes to the implementation timeline.

> **Review Feedback**

### Why it might work

It transforms administrative state into plain-language guidance.

This is especially strong for:

* first-time users;
* cognitive accessibility;
* unfamiliar terminology;
* next-action clarity.

### Risk

Too much conversational text could slow expert users.

### Crazy 8s verdict

**Excellent communication layer, but not enough as the whole information architecture.**

---

# The first Crazy 8s clustering

The eight ideas naturally form four clusters.

## Cluster A: Orientation

**1. Project Timeline**
**4. Journey Map Workspace**

They answer:

> **Where am I in the overall journey?**

---

## Cluster B: Immediate Action

**2. What Happens Next?**
**8. Conversational Project Guide**

They answer:

> **What do I need to understand or do now?**

---

## Cluster C: History and Continuity

**3. Project Story**

It answers:

> **What happened to my project?**

---

## Cluster D: Project as the Main Container

**5. Project Checklist**
**6. Project Control Center**
**7. One Project, One Page**

They answer:

> **Where does everything about this project belong?**

---

# Concept synthesis: the strongest idea is not one of the eight

This is exactly what Crazy 8s is supposed to produce.

The strongest concept is a combination:

## The Project Workspace

**One Project, One Workspace** becomes the structural foundation.

At the top:

### Project identity

> Community Digital Literacy Program

> **Current stage: Director/Admin Review**

Then three layers:

### Layer 1: Immediate reality

From Ideas 2, 6, and 8:

> **No action required**

> Your proposal is currently being reviewed by the Extension Services Office.

> Last activity: RET Chair endorsement, July 3

> Next: Director/Admin decision

### Layer 2: Overall journey

From Idea 1:

**Proposal** ✓ → **Review** ● → **Readiness** ○ → **Implementation** ○ → **Reporting** ○ → **Closure** ○

### Layer 3: Contextual work

The workspace changes according to the current stage.

During proposal preparation:

> Requirements and documents

During revision:

> Feedback and revised files

During readiness:

> Activation prerequisites

During implementation:

> Upcoming obligations

During reporting:

> Current report requirements

During closure:

> Completion requirements

Then persistent supporting areas:

**Overview | Documents | Feedback | Reports | Activity**

This is much stronger than simply creating separate:

> Proposal Module
> Project Module
> Report Module

for faculty users.

# Crazy 8s Round 2: The Right Action at the Right Time

Our second foundational HMW is:

> **How might we help every user immediately understand what requires their action, what they are waiting for, and who currently owns the next step?**

Here are eight rapid concepts.

## 1. Needs Your Action Inbox

One global list:

> **Needs Your Action**

> Review Proposal — 2 days waiting

> Submit Revision — due in 5 days

> Upload Progress Report — due in 12 days

The user processes work like an inbox.

**Strength:** clear and familiar.
**Risk:** can become an overwhelming task dump.

---

## 2. Three-State Work Model

Every item belongs to exactly one human state:

### Act

You need to do something.

### Wait

Someone else needs to do something.

### Watch

No action is currently required, but you are responsible for oversight.

This is conceptually interesting.

For example, the same proposal can be:

**Faculty:** Wait
**RET Chair:** Wait
**Director/Admin:** Act

### Verdict

**Extremely strong cross-role mental model.**

---

## 3. Ownership Badge

Every workflow item visibly says:

> **Waiting for: Director/Admin**

or:

> **Action required from you**

or:

> **Waiting for: Project Leader**

### Verdict

Simple, cheap, powerful.

This should probably become a universal design rule.

---

## 4. Responsibility Baton

Imagine the project as a relay.

The “baton” currently belongs to one actor:

**Faculty** → **RET Chair** → **Director/Admin** → **Faculty**

The UI always shows:

> **Current responsibility: Director/Admin**

### Strength

Excellent conceptual metaphor.

### Risk

Real workflows may have multiple simultaneous responsibilities.

### Verdict

Useful mental model, perhaps too literal for the UI.

---

## 5. Daily Briefing

Instead of a dashboard:

> **Good morning. Here's what changed.**

> 3 items need your action.

> 2 projects became overdue.

> 1 proposal you endorsed was approved.

### Strength

Reduces search.

### Risk

Can become notification noise.

---

## 6. Action-First Project Cards

Every project card leads with action, not status.

Weak:

> Community Digital Literacy
> Status: Returned

Better:

> **Revision required**

> Community Digital Literacy

> Address Director/Admin feedback and resubmit.

### Verdict

**Very strong.**

Users act on meaning, not database states.

---

## 7. Blocker Board

For administrative users:

### Waiting for Faculty

### Waiting for RET Chair

### Waiting for Director/Admin

### System / Requirement Blocked

Projects are grouped by ownership.

### Strength

Excellent for finding bottlenecks.

### Risk

Could become a complex Kanban board.

---

## 8. “Why am I seeing this?” Context

Every action explains:

> **Why this needs you**

> You are the RET Chair for CICT, and this proposal requires college endorsement before it can proceed.

### Strength

Excellent for multi-role users and accessibility.

### Risk

May be unnecessary for frequent users if always visible.

---

# Synthesis of Round 2

Again, the winner is a combination:

## Universal Action Model

Every workflow item should expose four properties:

**State:** Act / Wait / Watch

**Owner:** Who acts next?

**Reason:** Why is this happening?

**Next transition:** What happens after the action?

For example:

> **ACT — Revision required**

> Owner: You

> Why: Director/Admin requested changes to the implementation timeline.

> Next: Your revised proposal returns directly to Director/Admin review.

Or:

> **WAIT — Director/Admin review**

> Owner: Extension Services Office

> You do not need to do anything.

> Next: You will be notified when a decision is made.

This could become one of the strongest interaction principles in the entire system.

# What Ideate has produced so far

We now have two major product concepts:

## Concept 1: Project Workspace

The project—not the module—is the center of the user's experience.

## Concept 2: Act / Wait / Watch

Every workflow state is translated into the user's relationship with it.

Together:

> **One continuous project workspace that always tells the user whether to act, wait, or watch.**

That concept directly responds to the strongest findings from Empathize and Define without merely copying the original use cases. 

The next Crazy 8s rounds should tackle the remaining hard design challenges: **proposal preparation and revision**, **attention management for RET Chair and Director/Admin**, **project readiness from approval to activation**, and **preventive compliance**. Those are where the more difficult product decisions are hiding.
