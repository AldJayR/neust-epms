Yes. At this point, we should identify pain points **across the entire journey**, not merely per use case. The goal is to find where users experience **uncertainty, delay, unnecessary effort, cognitive load, loss of visibility, or unclear responsibility**.

These are still **hypothesized pain points** until validated through interviews and usability testing. The use cases tell us where friction *may* occur; users will tell us whether it actually does. 

# Core pain points across the extension-project journey

## 1. Users may not know what is required before they begin

The faculty journey currently starts with submitting proposal documents. UC-4 assumes that the user already knows:

* what documents are required;
* which templates to use;
* what information must be prepared;
* whether the submission is complete.

The system enters the journey too late.

### User experience

> “What exactly do I need to prepare?”

> “Is this the latest template?”

> “Am I missing anything?”

### Root problem

**Requirements uncertainty before submission.**

### Consequences

Users may rely on:

* colleagues;
* Messenger;
* email;
* old files;
* previous proposals;
* verbal instructions.

The result may be incomplete or incorrect submissions before the formal workflow even begins.

### Severity

**High**

Because errors introduced here create downstream work for the Faculty, RET Chair, and Director/Admin.

---

## 2. The project lifecycle may be difficult to understand

The system divides the journey into proposals, evaluation, projects, MOAs, Special Orders, reports, and closure. 

That makes sense architecturally.

It may not make sense to the user.

### User experience

> “My proposal was approved. Where did it go?”

> “Is this still a proposal or already a project?”

> “What happens after approval?”

### Root problem

**The system's data model may become visible to the user as the navigation model.**

### Consequences

Users must understand the internal structure of the system to follow their own project.

### Severity

**Critical**

This pain point affects almost the entire system.

### UCD implication

The project should feel continuous:

**Preparation → Review → Approval → Readiness → Implementation → Reporting → Closure**

even if the backend uses separate records.

---

## 3. “Pending” does not tell users enough

Many workflow states involve waiting:

* pending account approval;
* pending RET Chair review;
* pending Director/Admin review;
* pending requirements;
* pending activation.

### User experience

> “Pending with whom?”

> “Since when?”

> “Is something wrong?”

> “Do I need to do anything?”

### Root problem

**Status without context.**

### Consequences

Users manually follow up because the system does not answer the questions they actually have.

### Severity

**Critical**

A useful status should communicate:

**Current stage**
**Current owner**
**Time entered**
**Last activity**
**User's required action**
**What happens next**

---

## 4. Waiting may be invisible

UC-6 routes proposals sequentially, but the faculty member may only experience a period of silence. 

### User experience

> “Has anyone opened it?”

> “Is it stuck?”

> “Should I follow up?”

### Root problem

**Lack of process visibility during handoffs.**

### Consequences

* anxiety;
* repeated inquiries;
* unnecessary messages;
* duplicated coordination work;
* distrust in the system.

### Severity

**High**

The system should reduce the need for:

> “Any update po?”

---

## 5. Users may not know who owns the next action

This occurs repeatedly.

After submission:

> Faculty or RET Chair?

After endorsement:

> RET Chair or Director/Admin?

After approval:

> Faculty, RET Chair, or Director/Admin?

For Special Orders:

> Who uploads them?

For MOAs:

> Who acts next?

### Root problem

**Ambiguous action ownership.**

### Consequences

Everyone can see that something is incomplete, but nobody knows who is expected to resolve it.

### Severity

**Critical**

Every active workflow item should answer:

> **Who needs to act next?**

---

## 6. Returned proposals may create revision confusion

UC-4 and UC-6 contain relatively sophisticated resubmission logic. A proposal previously endorsed by the RET Chair can return directly to the Director/Admin after revision. 

But the user's experience may still be:

> “What exactly am I supposed to change?”

> “Which file?”

> “Do I upload everything again?”

> “Where does it go after I resubmit?”

### Root problem

**Feedback may not be sufficiently actionable.**

### Consequences

* incorrect revisions;
* unnecessary rework;
* repeated return cycles;
* frustration;
* longer approval time.

### Severity

**Critical**

The system should connect:

**Feedback → affected document → required change → revised version → next review stage**

---

## 7. Users may not understand what changed between versions

Reviewers also experience revision friction.

### RET Chair or Director/Admin experience

> “What did they actually revise?”

> “Do I need to read everything again?”

### Root problem

**Weak revision visibility.**

### Consequences

Reviewers may repeatedly inspect unchanged material.

### Severity

**High**

The system needs a clear submission history:

**Version 1**
Returned by RET Chair

**Version 2**
Endorsed by RET Chair
Returned by Director/Admin

**Version 3**
Current submission

Even without automatic document diffing, clear version history and change notes could significantly reduce cognitive load.

---

## 8. Approval may be mistaken for permission to begin implementation

This is one of the strongest pain points revealed by the journey.

UC-7 requires additional prerequisites after proposal approval, including Special Orders, MOA assignment, and a reporting schedule before activation. 

### Faculty experience

> “The project was approved. Can we start now?”

### Root problem

**Approval and activation are conceptually different, but may feel identical to users.**

### Consequences

Potentially:

* premature implementation;
* confusion;
* repeated questions;
* administrative noncompliance.

### Severity

**Critical**

The system needs to explicitly distinguish:

**Proposal Approved**

from:

**Project Authorized for Implementation**

And show what remains between them.

---

## 9. Users may not know what is blocking project activation

An approved project can still be missing:

* Special Orders;
* valid MOA;
* reporting schedule.

### User experience

> “Why is this still not ongoing?”

### Root problem

**Hidden prerequisites.**

### Consequences

Users see a stalled project but cannot diagnose the reason.

### Severity

**Critical**

The system needs a readiness model:

**Proposal approval** ✓
**Special Orders** ✓
**Valid MOA** ✕
**Reporting schedule** ✓

> **Blocked by: No valid MOA assigned**

The user should never need to reverse-engineer why a project cannot proceed.

---

## 10. RET Chairs face severe context switching

The RET Chair is simultaneously:

* a proposal evaluator;
* a college monitor;
* a faculty coordinator;
* potentially a project leader.

The specifications place this actor across most of the system. 

### User experience

> “Am I looking at my own projects or projects I oversee?”

### Root problem

**Multiple mental modes within one role.**

### Consequences

* cognitive overload;
* navigation mistakes;
* difficulty prioritizing;
* mixing personal work with oversight work.

### Severity

**High**

The UX should clearly separate:

**My Projects**

from:

**College Oversight**

and:

**Needs My Action**

---

## 11. Work may be hidden inside modules instead of surfaced as actions

A user may have to visit:

* Proposals;
* Projects;
* Reports;
* MOAs;

just to discover whether something needs attention.

### User experience

> “What do I need to do today?”

### Root problem

**Navigation-centered rather than task-centered UX.**

### Consequences

Important work is discovered only when users remember where to look.

### Severity

**Critical for RET Chair and Director/Admin**

The landing experience should surface:

* awaiting your review;
* returned and resubmitted;
* blocked;
* due soon;
* overdue;
* expiring soon.

---

## 12. Dashboards may show information without helping users decide

UC-3 focuses heavily on aggregated metrics and includes ranking faculty by project count. 

### User experience

Potentially:

> “Okay, there are 37 projects. What am I supposed to do with that?”

### Root problem

**Metrics without decision context.**

### Consequences

The dashboard becomes decorative rather than operational.

### Severity

**Medium to High**

The better question is not:

> “What data can we visualize?”

It is:

> “What decision is the user trying to make?”

---

## 13. Faculty ranking by project count may misrepresent performance

The current Director overview ranks faculty by number of extension projects. 

### Potential problem

More projects do not necessarily mean:

* greater impact;
* better project quality;
* stronger community outcomes;
* greater workload;
* better compliance.

### User experience risk

Faculty may perceive the system as turning extension participation into a leaderboard.

### Root problem

**A system metric is being treated as a performance measure without validated user need.**

### Severity

**High if used for decision-making**

This feature requires direct validation with the Director/Admin group.

---

## 14. MOA problems may be detected too late

UC-7 detects an MOA after it has expired. 

### Director/Admin experience

> “Why am I only finding out now?”

### Root problem

**Reactive rather than preventive monitoring.**

### Consequences

* project disruption;
* urgent administrative work;
* compliance risk.

### Severity

**High**

The pain occurs before expiration, so the system should support:

**Expiring in 90 days**
**Expiring in 30 days**
**Expired**

---

## 15. Overdue-report detection is also reactive

UC-7 detects overdue reports. 

But the Faculty journey suggests the user needs help **before** missing the deadline.

### Root problem

**The system reacts to failure instead of helping prevent it.**

### Better experience

**Due in 30 days**
**Due in 7 days**
**Due tomorrow**
**Overdue**

### Severity

**High**

---

## 16. Reporting may feel like a generic upload task

UC-8 begins when the user submits a report document. 

But users need to know:

* which report is due;
* when it is due;
* what documents are required;
* whether it was successfully submitted.

### Root problem

**The system models reports as records rather than obligations in time.**

### Severity

**High**

The experience should be:

> **Progress Report 2**

> Due August 30

> Not yet submitted

not:

> **Upload Report**

---

## 17. Automatic project closure may not match the real workflow

UC-8 closes the project after the Terminal Report and Final Accomplishment Report are recorded. 

### Potential pain

If those documents require verification or acceptance, automatic closure could create an inaccurate state.

### Root problem

**The specification may confuse document submission with institutional completion.**

### Severity

**Potentially Critical**

This must be validated against the actual Extension Services workflow.

---

# The pain points cluster into six deeper problems

The individual issues are symptoms. Underneath them, I see six systemic pain clusters:

**Uncertainty** — What do I need? Where is it? What happens next?

**Visibility** — Who has it? How long has it been there? What changed?

**Ownership** — Who is responsible for the next action?

**Continuity** — Why does one project feel fragmented across proposals, MOAs, projects, and reports?

**Cognitive load** — What needs my attention among all these projects?

**Prevention** — Why does the system tell me only after something has expired or become overdue?

These six clusters are much more useful for the Design Thinking process than a list of 17 disconnected feature complaints.

The next step in **Define** should be to turn these into precise **problem statements and “How Might We” questions**. That will let us distinguish genuine user problems from premature feature ideas.
