Yes. This should be a **second lens across the entire Design Thinking process**, not an accessibility checklist added after the UI is finished.

The question changes from:

> **How do we make the workflow easier for the average user?**

to:

> **Who may still be excluded, disadvantaged, slowed down, embarrassed, or given less power by the workflow we are designing?**

For your Extension Services system, I would run an **Accessibility and Equity-Focused Design Thinking process** in parallel with the existing one:

**Empathize inclusively → Define exclusion → Ideate for equitable participation → Prototype accessibly → Test with diverse users**

The current use cases already reveal several places where unequal access or unequal burden could emerge: self-registration, document submission, proposal review, dashboards, notifications, MOA management, reporting deadlines, and role-based authority. 

# 1. Empathize inclusively

Our previous personas represented typical role-based users:

* Faculty / Project Leader
* RET Chair
* Director/Admin
* Super Admin

For accessibility and equity, we now ask:

> **How does the same journey change under different human circumstances?**

We should not create stereotypical “disabled personas.” Instead, we examine the **same personas under different access conditions**.

## Faculty / Project Leader: possible access conditions

The Faculty Project Leader may:

* use a phone more often than a desktop;
* have unreliable or slow internet;
* have low digital confidence;
* use keyboard-only navigation;
* use a screen reader or magnification;
* have difficulty distinguishing certain colors;
* have limited time between classes;
* be interrupted during long forms;
* be unfamiliar with administrative terminology;
* have temporary limitations, such as an injured hand or eye strain;
* work in a campus or community location with poor connectivity.

The empathy question becomes:

> **Can this faculty member complete the same project journey with comparable independence and confidence?**

Not:

> “Can they technically access the page?”

That distinction matters.

---

## RET Chair: possible access conditions

The RET Chair may:

* oversee many projects simultaneously;
* also be a project leader;
* use the system between meetings and classes;
* experience high cognitive load;
* work primarily from a laptop but occasionally from a phone;
* have age-related changes in vision or motor precision;
* rely on scanning rather than carefully reading every screen.

The accessibility problem is therefore not only disability access.

It is also:

* cognitive accessibility;
* workload accessibility;
* information prioritization;
* error prevention.

A technically WCAG-conforming screen can still be inaccessible if the RET Chair must remember the state of 40 projects.

---

## Director/Admin: possible access conditions

The Director/Admin may:

* process a large number of records;
* delegate work across authorized staff;
* work under deadline pressure;
* use different devices;
* need to retrieve old records quickly;
* have varying levels of technical confidence within the same role group.

An important equity question appears here:

> **Does the interface assume that all Director/Admin users possess the same authority?**

Because your role group combines the Director and authorized Extension Services staff, equal access to the module should not automatically mean equal authority to make every decision.

The system should distinguish:

**Ability to perform administrative work**

from:

**Authority to make institutional decisions**

That is an equity and governance concern.

---

# 2. Identify exclusion points in the current journey

Now we revisit the journey maps specifically looking for exclusion.

# Exclusion Point 1: Proposal preparation may privilege experienced insiders

A faculty member who has previously submitted extension proposals may already know:

* the requirements;
* the terminology;
* the templates;
* who to ask;
* the unofficial process.

A first-time project leader may not.

### Equity problem

Two users with equally strong project ideas may have unequal chances of successful submission because one has more **institutional knowledge**.

### Excluded or disadvantaged users

* first-time project leaders;
* newly hired faculty;
* faculty unfamiliar with extension procedures;
* users from units with less experienced support networks.

### Design principle

> **The system should not require insider knowledge to complete an official process.**

### Design response

Provide:

* explicit requirements;
* current templates;
* plain-language explanations;
* examples where appropriate;
* contextual help;
* visible completeness;
* clear next steps.

This turns procedural knowledge from a social advantage into shared institutional information.

---

# Exclusion Point 2: File-heavy workflows may privilege desktop users and strong internet connections

The current use cases rely heavily on document submission across proposals, Special Orders, MOAs, progress reports, and closure reports. 

### Equity problem

A user with:

* fast internet;
* a modern laptop;
* stable connectivity;

has a fundamentally easier experience than someone working through:

* mobile data;
* intermittent Wi-Fi;
* an older device;
* a remote community location.

### User experience

> “I uploaded this for ten minutes and it failed.”

> “Do I have to start again?”

> “Did the file actually submit?”

### Design responses

The system should:

* preserve drafts;
* avoid losing completed form data after upload failure;
* show upload progress;
* allow individual file retry;
* clearly show successful and failed uploads;
* use reasonable file-size limits;
* avoid unnecessary re-uploading;
* preserve already submitted documents during revisions;
* warn before a session expires;
* support resumable workflows even if uploads themselves cannot technically resume.

### Equity principle

> **A temporary connection failure should not erase a user's work.**

---

# Exclusion Point 3: Long forms may punish users who need more time

Suppose proposal creation involves a long session.

Some users may need more time because they:

* use assistive technology;
* type slowly;
* need to consult documents;
* are interrupted by classes;
* experience cognitive fatigue;
* are unfamiliar with the process.

### Equity problem

A session timeout affects these users disproportionately.

### Design responses

* autosave drafts;
* allow users to leave and continue later;
* preserve uploaded files;
* provide a visible save state;
* warn before timeout;
* do not impose unnecessarily short completion windows.

### Design principle

> **Time should not become an invisible accessibility barrier.**

---

# Exclusion Point 4: Color-only statuses exclude some users

Your workflow contains many states:

* pending;
* returned;
* endorsed;
* approved;
* blocked;
* ongoing;
* overdue;
* expired;
* completed. 

A tempting design is:

**Green = approved**
**Yellow = pending**
**Red = overdue**

That is insufficient.

### Accessibility problem

Users with color-vision differences may not distinguish status reliably.

### Design response

Never use color alone.

Use:

**✓ Approved**

**○ Awaiting Review**

**! Action Required**

**⚠ Overdue**

with:

* text labels;
* icons;
* sufficient contrast;
* consistent patterns.

Color should reinforce meaning, not carry meaning by itself.

---

# Exclusion Point 5: A visual project timeline may exclude screen-reader users

Earlier, we proposed a project lifecycle:

**Submitted ✓ → RET Chair Review → Director/Admin Review → Approved**

Visually, that is useful.

But if implemented carelessly, a screen reader may encounter:

> circle, line, circle, checkmark, line

### Accessibility requirement

The same information must have a meaningful semantic structure.

For example:

> Current stage: Director/Admin Review.

> Completed stages: Proposal Submitted; RET Chair Endorsed.

> Next stage: Approval Decision.

The visual timeline and accessible text representation must communicate equivalent information.

### Principle

> **The accessible version should not be an inferior summary of the visual version.**

---

# Exclusion Point 6: Vague administrative language creates cognitive barriers

Terms such as:

* pending;
* validated;
* endorsed;
* activated;
* implementation update;
* prerequisite status;
* reporting schedule;
* project completion status;

may be institutionally accurate but not always self-explanatory. 

### User experience

> “What does endorsed mean?”

> “Approved but not active?”

> “What am I supposed to do?”

### Design response

Keep official terminology where required, but explain it.

Instead of:

> **Status: Endorsed**

show:

> **Endorsed by RET Chair**

> Your proposal has been forwarded to the Extension Services office for review.

Instead of:

> **Status: Approved**

show:

> **Proposal Approved**

> The proposal has been approved, but the project is not yet authorized for implementation.

### Equity principle

> **Understanding administrative language should not depend on prior experience with the institution.**

---

# Exclusion Point 7: Notifications may disadvantage users who cannot constantly check the system

Some users may open the portal daily.

Others may not.

If important actions exist only inside the dashboard, frequent users gain an advantage.

### Equity problem

The system rewards constant checking.

### Design response

Meaningful events should reach users through appropriate channels.

The notification should communicate:

**What happened**
**Which project**
**Whether action is required**
**What action is required**
**When it is due**

For example:

> **Revision required for Community Digital Literacy Program**

> The Director/Admin returned your proposal with requested changes.

> Action required: Submit a revised proposal.

A notification that merely says:

> **Proposal status updated**

creates unnecessary cognitive and navigational burden.

---

# Exclusion Point 8: Deadline design may punish users instead of supporting them

UC-7 detects overdue reports after the deadline has already been missed. 

That is procedurally equal:

> Everyone has the same deadline.

But it is not necessarily equitably designed.

### Equality

Everyone receives the same due date.

### Equity

Everyone receives sufficient information and advance warning to have a fair opportunity to meet it.

### Design response

Provide progressive reminders:

* upcoming;
* due soon;
* due tomorrow;
* overdue.

And always state:

* what is due;
* for which project;
* the deadline;
* the required action.

### Principle

> **A good system helps users avoid failure before documenting that they failed.**

---

# Exclusion Point 9: Faculty ranking may create structural inequity

The current Director dashboard includes ranking faculty by project count. 

This deserves stronger scrutiny under an equity lens.

Different faculty members may have different:

* teaching loads;
* administrative assignments;
* project complexity;
* access to partner communities;
* disciplinary opportunities;
* team structures.

A raw project count can flatten those differences.

### Equity risk

The system may make unequal circumstances look like unequal effort.

### Design question

> **What decision will this ranking support?**

If there is no clear and fair answer, the ranking should not exist.

A more equitable overview might show:

* participation;
* project status;
* distribution across colleges;
* completion;
* community reach;
* compliance;

without turning faculty into a leaderboard.

---

# Exclusion Point 10: One account may not equal one role

The current specifications assign roles and include special handling for the RET Chair, who may also submit proposals. 

### Equity and access problem

A person may simultaneously be:

* an RET Chair;
* a project leader;
* a project member.

Forcing one identity into one role can restrict legitimate participation.

### Design response

The system should distinguish:

**Who the person is**

from:

**What responsibility they are performing in this context**

The RET Chair should not lose the faculty project experience simply because they have an oversight role.

This supports the earlier design:

**My Projects**

and

**College Oversight**

without requiring separate accounts.

---

# 3. Accessibility-focused user stories

We can now add accessibility stories to the backlog.

## Perceivable information

> **As a user with low vision, I want text and interface elements to have sufficient contrast so that I can read and distinguish information.**

> **As a user who cannot reliably distinguish colors, I want statuses to use text and other visual indicators in addition to color so that I can understand project states.**

> **As a screen-reader user, I want project stages, status changes, form errors, and notifications to be announced meaningfully so that I receive the same essential information as sighted users.**

> **As a user who enlarges content, I want the interface to remain usable when text is zoomed so that I do not lose information or functionality.**

## Operable interaction

> **As a keyboard user, I want to complete every essential workflow without a mouse so that I can independently use the system.**

> **As a user with limited motor precision, I want interactive controls to be sufficiently large and separated so that I can avoid accidental actions.**

> **As a user completing a long workflow, I want my work preserved so that additional time or interruptions do not cause data loss.**

## Understandable workflows

> **As a first-time project leader, I want unfamiliar administrative terms explained in context so that I can complete the process without relying on insider knowledge.**

> **As a user who makes an error, I want the system to explain what went wrong and how to correct it so that I can recover independently.**

> **As a user making a consequential decision, I want to understand what will happen before I confirm it so that I can avoid serious mistakes.**

## Robust access

> **As a user of assistive technology, I want controls, forms, status messages, and navigation to use meaningful semantics so that the system works reliably with my tools.**

These should not be a separate “accessibility feature” epic that can be cut from the MVP.

They are acceptance conditions across the core product.

# 4. Equity-focused user stories

> **As a first-time project leader, I want the same procedural guidance available to experienced faculty so that my success does not depend on knowing the right person to ask.**

> **As a user with an unstable internet connection, I want my completed work preserved when connectivity fails so that I do not have to repeat the entire process.**

> **As a user who cannot check the system frequently, I want meaningful notifications about actions and deadlines so that I am not disadvantaged by infrequent portal access.**

> **As an RET Chair who is also a project leader, I want to access both my personal project responsibilities and my oversight responsibilities without maintaining separate accounts.**

> **As an authorized Extension Services staff member, I want access appropriate to my actual responsibilities so that I can perform administrative work without receiving decision-making authority I should not have.**

> **As a faculty member, I want institutional activity data to be presented in context rather than as a simplistic leaderboard so that differences in circumstances are not misrepresented as differences in performance.**

# 5. Inclusive design principles for the entire system

From this analysis, I would establish these as product-level principles:

1. **No insider knowledge required.** Official processes must be understandable from the system itself.

2. **No color-only meaning.** Every status must remain understandable without color.

3. **No mouse-only workflow.** Every essential task must be keyboard operable.

4. **No punishment for taking longer.** Long workflows must preserve progress.

5. **No data loss from ordinary connection failure.** Users should recover without starting over.

6. **No unexplained administrative terminology.** Official terms should be paired with plain-language meaning.

7. **No status without action context.** Users should know who acts next and whether they need to do anything.

8. **No reactive-only compliance design.** The system should warn before deadlines and expirations.

9. **No simplistic performance ranking without context.** Metrics must support fair and legitimate decisions.

10. **No assumption that role equals person.** Users may hold multiple responsibilities.

11. **No inaccessible alternative experience.** Assistive-technology users should receive equivalent information and functionality.

12. **No accessibility as a post-development phase.** It must shape requirements, stories, prototypes, and testing.

The strongest outcome of this lens is that accessibility and equity **change the product requirements themselves**. They are not merely visual requirements like contrast and font size.

They affect whether you need autosave, how notifications work, whether ranking should exist, how multi-role users are modeled, when deadline warnings occur, how proposal requirements are communicated, and whether a connection failure destroys work.

The next artifact should be an **Accessibility and Equity Risk Matrix** for the entire project journey—identifying each barrier, who is disproportionately affected, severity, current use case involved, and the design requirement needed to mitigate it.
